01 ‚Äî The Research Foundation

What the Science Says

A 2025 systematic review published in Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience analyzed 13 studies on boxing exercise for Parkinson's disease and found consistent evidence that boxing improves gait, mobility, and balance in people with PD. The majority of these studies were published between 2020 and 2023 ‚Äî this is a rapidly growing and strengthening body of evidence.

Most importantly, high-intensity exercise like boxing may actually slow Parkinson's progression by promoting neuroplasticity ‚Äî the brain's ability to form new connections and adapt. This places boxing in a unique category: not just symptom management, but potentially disease-modifying.

Boxing & The Brain: Neuroplasticity

Parkinson's disease is caused by the progressive loss of dopamine-producing neurons. While medication manages symptoms, exercise is the only intervention shown to potentially promote neuroplasticity ‚Äî meaning it may help the brain compensate and adapt at a structural level.

Boxing is classified as high-intensity exercise. Research strongly suggests that high-intensity exercise promotes neuroplasticity in people with PD in ways that low-intensity exercise does not. This is why the intensity of our classes is not just about fitness ‚Äî it's neurologically purposeful.

A 2023 pilot study from Rush University Medical Center (Patel et al.) found that people with PD who completed a 12-week community-based boxing program showed improvements in both motor and non-motor symptoms ‚Äî including mood, sleep, and cognitive function.

03 ‚Äî Mental Health & Wellbeing

üßò Stress Relief & Mood

Hitting a heavy bag is one of the most instinctively satisfying physical releases available. Beyond the feeling, intense exercise triggers a sustained release of endorphins, dopamine, and serotonin ‚Äî the exact neurotransmitters that PD depletes. A boxing class isn't just exercise; it's a neurochemical reset.

Patel et al. (2023) found non-motor symptom improvements including mood following a 12-week boxing program. Wu et al. found exercise significantly reduces depressive symptoms in PD populations.

üò¥ Sleep

Poor sleep is a common and debilitating non-motor symptom of PD. Regular high-intensity exercise is one of the most evidence-supported non-pharmacological interventions for sleep quality. A 2020 pilot study by Urrutia et al. specifically examined boxing's effect on sleep in PD and found positive improvements in both sleep quality and daytime sleepiness.

Urrutia et al. (2020) ‚Äî Topics in Geriatric Rehabilitation: Boxing to improve sleep quality and daytime sleepiness in PD.

ü§ù Community & Belonging

A 2024 qualitative study from Denmark found that participants in Parkinson's boxing programs reported a strong sense of community, joy, gratitude, and improved mental wellbeing ‚Äî not just physical improvement. The shared diagnosis, shared effort, and shared victories created bonds that participants described as profoundly meaningful.

Danish Parkinson Boxing qualitative study, Disability & Rehabilitation (published online May 2024).

üí° Cognitive Function

The dual-task nature of boxing ‚Äî simultaneously moving, thinking, reacting, and counting ‚Äî places significant demands on executive function and working memory. Regular dual-task training has been shown to improve cognitive performance in older adults and people with PD, slowing the decline that often accompanies the disease.

Multiple included studies tracked cognitive outcomes via the MDS-UPDRS III and PDQ-39, showing improvements in cognitive subscores following boxing programs.

04 ‚Äî B4H Techniques: What Makes Us Different

We teach boxing ‚Äî but our approach is intentionally different from traditional boxing technique. Understanding why makes you a better instructor.

Traditional Boxing

  • Hands always up ‚Äî chin down, elbows in
  • Rounded, protective shoulders ‚Äî guards the face
  • Constant tension ‚Äî ready to absorb or deflect blows
  • Compact, closed posture ‚Äî minimizes target area
  • Great for the ring ‚Äî works against PD clients

Boxing 4 Health Approach

  • Strong, upright posture ‚Äî chest open, spine tall
  • Opening movements throughout ‚Äî counteract PD's forward flexion
  • Full extension on punches ‚Äî amplitude is the goal
  • Relaxed between combinations ‚Äî reduce rigidity, not add to it
  • High intensity ‚Äî without reinforcing poor posture

ü•ä The Key Principle: People with Parkinson's already fight against forward flexion, rounded shoulders, and stooped posture every day. The last thing we want is to design a class that reinforces those patterns. Every opening stretch, every tall stance cue, every full extension punch is intentionally working against the postural pull of PD ‚Äî not with it.
