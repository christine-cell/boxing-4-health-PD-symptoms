02 ‚Äî Depression, Anxiety & Community

Community Is Medicine

10‚Äì15% of seniors in Canada suffer from depression. It's especially common when big life changes occur ‚Äî retirement, sudden loss, downsizing, or a health diagnosis. Depression is associated with increased risk of cardiac disease, increased risk of death from illness, and significantly reduced ability to rehabilitate.

But here's what matters for you as an instructor: the community you build around your classes is one of the most powerful antidepressants available. The friendships formed, the shared laughter, the sense of belonging and purpose ‚Äî these aren't side effects of your program. They're core outcomes.

Cognitive Decline & Dementia

The ability to think, learn, and remember are key components of brain health. Physical exercise is one of the most powerful tools we have to reduce risk of cognitive impairment and dementia. Studies show exercise modifies metabolic, structural, and functional dimensions of the brain ‚Äî preserving cognitive performance in older adults.

ü•ä Your dual-task exercises ‚Äî moving AND counting AND following instructions simultaneously ‚Äî are literally brain training. Remind clients of this!

Anxiety in Seniors

Anxiety often travels alongside depression in older adults. Fear of falling, fear of losing independence, social isolation, and grief are all common anxiety triggers in seniors. Exercise reduces cortisol, increases endorphins, and ‚Äî critically ‚Äî gives people a reason to leave the house and be around others.

ü•ä A warm, consistent welcome at the door matters more than you know. For some clients, your class may be the highlight of their week.

What to Know

  • Recovery is specific to each individual ‚Äî no two knees are the same
  • Client must be cleared by their physician before returning to higher intensity classes (approx. 3‚Äì6 months)
  • The more exercise they do in weeks following surgery, the better their outcome

Osteoporosis
Bone Health
‚ñº

What to Know

  • Significantly increases fracture risk from impact or sudden jarring
  • Very common in older women, but affects men too
  • Often undiagnosed ‚Äî always ask about bone density history
  • Compression fractures in the spine can occur from seemingly minor incidents

Class Adaptations

  • Avoid running, jumping, or jerky movements
  • No high-impact activities
  • Focus on controlled, smooth, deliberate movement
  • All plyometric exercises must be eliminated or heavily modified
  • Weighted resistance exercises are actually beneficial ‚Äî bone responds to load
ü•ä Silver Lining: Resistance training and weight-bearing exercise (like boxing punches) are actually clinically recommended for osteoporosis ‚Äî they stimulate bone formation. Your program is beneficial, just avoid the impact elements.

Check in to see how they feel throughout class AND afterward ‚Äî delayed soreness is common. A meaningful milestone goal for both knee and hip replacements is getting down to the ground and back up independently ‚Äî but take the time needed to get there safely and celebrate every step of that journey.
