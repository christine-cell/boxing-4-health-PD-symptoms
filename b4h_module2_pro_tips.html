Water Breaks ‚Äî Insert Freely, Remind Often
Insert water breaks any time during the session when you feel the class needs one ‚Äî don't wait for a designated moment. This population is particularly prone to dehydration, and many PD clients experience reduced thirst sensation, meaning they genuinely won't feel thirsty even when they need water. Friendly reminders about the importance of staying hydrated ‚Äî even when not thirsty ‚Äî are an essential part of every class. Make it habit: "Let's grab some water before we keep going."

Music Is a Therapeutic Tool ‚Äî Use It Intentionally
Music is a great motivator ‚Äî and for Parkinson's clients specifically, it does far more than set the mood. Rhythm externally cues movement, which directly addresses the internal timing deficits that PD creates. Have fun building your playlists, but also use them strategically: higher BPM for high-intensity circuits, slower rhythm for cool-down and release, strong beat for boxing combinations. The right song at the right moment can transform a struggling client. The Boxing 4 Health Spotify playlist is a great starting point ‚Äî build on it and make it yours.

Technique Revisions ‚Äî Maximum 2‚Äì3 Per Class
Resist the urge to correct everything you see. Limit technique revisions to 2‚Äì3 per class ‚Äî any more overwhelms clients and actually reduces how much they retain. Choose the corrections that matter most for safety or therapeutic benefit and focus there. The best time to deliver a technique revision is during a water break ‚Äî the class momentum stays intact, clients are standing still and listening, and the lesson lands without interruption. Pick your moments wisely.

Use Images, Not Instructions ‚Äî Fewer Words, Bigger Impact
The fewer words used, the better. Try to convey an image in the student's mind rather than a technical explanation. A vivid mental image triggers the movement more naturally and completely than a list of instructions ever will.

‚ùå Too Many Words

‚Äî"We want high knee raises with the arms alternating reaching upwards in opposition to the leg."
‚Äî"Make sure your elbow is at 90 degrees and your fist is at shoulder height as you extend the opposite leg."
‚Äî"Keep your core engaged and your shoulders back while alternating the knee drive."

‚úì Image-Based Cues

‚Üí"Climb a ladder!"
‚Üí"Walk through deep snow!"
‚Üí"Reach for the ceiling!"
‚Üí"March like you own the room!"
Build your own library of image cues over time. The ones that make your clients laugh, light up, or suddenly move differently are the ones worth keeping.

Teach from Multiple Angles
Don't just stand at the front. Move around the room and teach from different angles ‚Äî in front, beside, behind, and from within the group. Seeing a movement from the side reveals the trunk rotation that's invisible from the front. Seeing it from behind shows foot position and hip alignment. Each angle reveals a different component of the movement, and different clients respond to different views. Varying your position also keeps clients visually engaged and aware ‚Äî they can't zone out if you might appear next to them at any moment.

Common Questions

Questions every new B4H instructor eventually asks ‚Äî answered clearly.

Q
How do I know if students are working hard enough?
‚ñº
Two reliable indicators: heart rate and the talk test.

Check their target heart rate periodically using the 10-second count and THR chart ‚Äî if they're below 70%, they need to push harder. The second test is simpler: if they can carry on a full, comfortable conversation during class, they aren't working hard enough. They should be able to say short sentences, but sustained conversation should feel difficult.

Sweat is a useful indicator for some clients ‚Äî but not all. PD clients may have altered sweating responses, and some medications affect perspiration, so don't rely on sweat alone as your gauge. Heart rate is your most objective measure ‚Äî use it.
Q
How do I know if a student is working too hard?
‚ñº
This is primarily the student's responsibility ‚Äî but they need constant reminders to listen to their body. Many clients are motivated to push through discomfort, which is admirable but can cross a line.

Watch for these patterns over time: if a client is consistently tired after class, frequently needs naps, or finds themselves more fatigued than usual in the days following a session ‚Äî they are working too hard. It's cumulative, not just about any one class.

If you notice a client who can't keep up over multiple sessions ‚Äî and it's getting worse rather than better ‚Äî don't try to manage this alone. Speak to your head instructor and flag it. There may be a medication change, a progression in symptoms, or another factor at play that needs professional attention.
Q
Do functional exercises always have to come before boxing?
‚ñº
No ‚Äî you can absolutely start with boxing. The Fighter and Champion templates are frameworks and starting points, not rigid rules. You can mix up the order, combine elements, run them simultaneously as circuit stations, or experiment with whatever sequence works best for your group on a given day.

The only non-negotiables are the warm-up before intensity and the cool-down after intensity. Everything in between is yours to shape. Bring your flair to it ‚Äî that's what makes your class yours.

For Physiotherapists
Running B4H Classes

Your clinical training gives you tools that most fitness instructors simply don't have. This section is for you ‚Äî ways to go deeper, assess more precisely, challenge more intentionally, and bring your full professional expertise into the B4H framework.

üìä
Formal Movement Assessments Within Class
Clinical Assessment ¬∑ Baseline & Progress
You can embed validated outcome measures directly into your sessions without disrupting the flow. Use the beginning or end of class to gather data that gives you a clinical picture of each client over time.
  • Timed Up and Go (TUG) ‚Äî a quick functional mobility marker. Run it at intake and every 4‚Äì6 weeks to track progress objectively.
  • 10-Metre Walk Test ‚Äî gait speed, stride length, and arm swing observation during warm-up walking can be done informally but consistently.
  • Berg Balance Scale (selected items) ‚Äî embed relevant balance tasks into your circuit stations and observe clinically while instructing.
  • Functional Reach Test ‚Äî a simple overhead reach or lateral reach during warm-up gives you a repeatable balance proxy.
  • Use your own clinical eye during every class. You notice things fitness instructors miss ‚Äî document what you see and use it to adapt your programming.

Advanced Dual-Task Programming
Cognitive Load ¬∑ Motor-Cognitive Challenge
You understand dual-task interference in PD at a neurological level. Use that knowledge to design more sophisticated cognitive-motor challenges that standard instructors wouldn't think to include.
  • Grade the cognitive load deliberately. Start with simple automatic tasks (humming) during movement, then progress to demanding tasks (serial 7s, verbal fluency) as clients improve.
  • Introduce task-switching. Have clients alternate between two different cognitive tasks mid-circuit ‚Äî this taxes executive function in a way that directly mirrors real-world PD challenges.
  • Use response inhibition tasks. "Punch when I say go, stop when I clap" ‚Äî stop-signal paradigms challenge the basal ganglia specifically.
  • Design obstacle courses using cones, floor markers, and stepping patterns that require visual scanning, decision-making, and motor execution simultaneously.
  • Progress clients individually based on their cognitive profile ‚Äî some will fatigue cognitively before physically. Track both.

Targeted Postural & Gait Interventions
Biomechanics ¬∑ Freezing ¬∑ Festination
Your biomechanical eye lets you identify and address specific gait and postural deviations that a fitness instructor would miss entirely. Use this.
  • Festination management: Incorporate visual cues (floor tape, stepping targets) and auditory cues (metronome, beat of music) specifically calibrated to interrupt festination patterns.
  • Freezing of Gait (FOG) strategies: Embed rhythm-based cueing, visual floor targets, attentional strategies, and large stepping practice into every session for clients with FOG history.
  • Camptocormia and trunk flexion: You can prescribe targeted thoracic extension and hip flexor release work in the cool-down that goes well beyond standard stretching.
  • Asymmetric presentation: PD is asymmetric. Assess which side is more affected and design unilateral exercises that load the weaker side intentionally.
  • Introduce perturbation training ‚Äî controlled balance challenges with a safety spotter that push reactive postural responses. This is clinical-grade fall prevention embedded in a boxing class.

Working Around Medication Timing (ON/OFF Cycles)
Levodopa ¬∑ ON Period ¬∑ Clinical Timing
You understand levodopa pharmacokinetics in a way that gives you a significant clinical advantage. Use it to optimise each client's session.
  • Schedule class timing around ON periods wherever possible. Speak to clients about when their medication peaks and structure the high-intensity portion of class accordingly.
  • For clients in an OFF period at class start: use the warm-up as a longer, gentler activation phase and hold the high-intensity work until you observe the medication beginning to take effect.
  • Educate clients on dose timing relative to exercise ‚Äî many don't know that exercise itself affects dopamine availability and can complement their medication.
  • Be aware of dyskinesia ‚Äî if a client is visibly overmedicated (involuntary movements), reduce intensity demands and adapt accordingly rather than pushing through.
  • Document ON/OFF patterns across sessions. Over time you'll build a clinical picture that helps you predict each client's best and most challenging windows.

Progressive Overload ‚Äî The Clinical Way
Programming ¬∑ Periodisation ¬∑ Individual Progression
You have the clinical training to apply proper progressive overload principles to a group exercise setting ‚Äî something that separates a physio-led class from a fitness-led one.
  • Keep individual progress notes. Track what each client is doing, what you modified, and where they struggled. Use this to design intentional progressions from session to session.
  • Apply the FITT principle (Frequency, Intensity, Time, Type) to individual clients within the group ‚Äî increase one variable at a time, not all at once.
  • Introduce load progressions with bands, light weights, and balance instability (foam pads, balance boards) for clients who are ready. The online format uses bodyweight ‚Äî your in-person setting gives you access to tools that make this richer.
  • Identify and name each client's primary impairment (rigidity, tremor, bradykinesia, postural instability) and design their circuit experience to target it most heavily.
  • Communicate with their neurologist or GP where appropriate. You are qualified to do this. A physio-led B4H class that integrates with a client's broader clinical care is genuinely exceptional.

Bring Your Creativity ‚Äî This Is Your Canvas
Clinical Creativity ¬∑ Beyond the Template
The B4H framework is a brilliant foundation. As a physiotherapist, you have the clinical knowledge to make it extraordinary. Don't limit yourself to the template ‚Äî it's permission to start, not a ceiling to stay under.
  • Bring in techniques from LSVT BIG, PD Warrior, Nordic Walking, Tai Chi, or dance therapy if you're trained in them ‚Äî they integrate beautifully with the B4H structure.
  • Design class themes around specific PD challenges ‚Äî a "turn and balance" day, a "voice and face" day, a "reaching and amplitude" day ‚Äî and go deep rather than broad.
  • Use your hands clinically. Physios are uniquely skilled at hands-on facilitation. Use this during cool-down to provide proper myofascial release, joint mobilisation, or postural facilitation ‚Äî not just massage ball rolling.
  • Share what works with the B4H instructor community. Your clinical innovations can raise the bar for every B4H class globally. Post in the Instructor Forum. Teach what you learn.
