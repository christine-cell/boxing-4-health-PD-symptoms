Cueing Technique 01
V

Vocal

Voice ¬∑ Clarity ¬∑ Energy ¬∑ Repetition
Using your voice in a clear and direct manner cuts through the noise and communicates your points effectively. For clients with Parkinson's ‚Äî who may be processing information more slowly, managing cognitive fog, or dealing with hearing changes ‚Äî clarity and repetition are everything.
Your clients will feed off the energy you bring to class. Take a moment to centre yourself before you begin, and then project as much excitement, positivity, and confidence as possible. Your mood is contagious ‚Äî if you're flat, the room is flat. If you're fired up, they're fired up.
1
Be Clear & Direct
No long explanations during movement. Short, punchy cues that land immediately: "Punch BIG!" "Step wider!" "Chin up!" The more words you use, the less they hear.
2
Embrace Repetition
Repeat the important points as many times as needed until the message gets across. What feels repetitive to you is often the first time it fully registers for a client managing cognitive symptoms. Never say something important just once.
3
Project Confidence & Positivity
Clients with PD often experience anxiety, self-doubt, and fear of falling. Your confident, positive tone creates psychological safety ‚Äî it tells them they're in good hands and that they can do this.
4
Centre Yourself First
If you're stressed, rushed, or distracted, your clients will feel it. Before class, take a breath, reset, and bring your best energy to the room. They deserve it ‚Äî and so do you.

Mirroring

Position ¬∑ Demonstration ¬∑ BIG Movement
Over and above a simple demonstration, mirroring the movement directly in front of a client is a vital tool ‚Äî especially for the most affected clients. When someone with Parkinson's can see exactly what they need to do, performed at full amplitude by their trusted instructor, it brings a sense of calm and comfort that words alone can't provide.

Mirroring means you move opposite to them. If you want them to raise their right hand, you raise your left ‚Äî so from their perspective, it looks correct. Position yourself directly in front of your client, make eye contact, and move together.
1
Position Directly in Front
Get face-to-face with the client you're cueing. Not beside them, not behind them ‚Äî directly in front so they can see your whole body and follow the movement without having to turn.
2
Make It as BIG as Possible
Exaggerate every movement. Full extension. Exaggerated steps. Maximum arm swing. Your amplitude sets the target ‚Äî they aim for you. If you mirror small, they move small.
3
Use It for Your Most Affected Clients
Mirroring is particularly powerful for clients who are struggling with a movement or having an OFF period. Drop everything else and get in front of them ‚Äî your body becomes their guide.
4
Create Calm & Confidence
Seeing their trusted instructor right in front of them, moving together, is deeply reassuring. It removes the guesswork and the anxiety of figuring out what to do ‚Äî they just follow you.

Physical Feedback

Touch ¬∑ Consent ¬∑ Awareness ¬∑ Range
The sense of touch, used alongside vocal and visual cues, can unlock movement that nothing else can reach. Something as simple as tapping on a body part can bring enough awareness to help a client find greater range, ability, and balance. For PD clients who have lost proprioceptive awareness, physical feedback can be the missing link.

‚ö†Ô∏è Consent is Non-Negotiable

Always establish consent before putting your hands on a client's body ‚Äî every single time. This is not optional. Physical contact is a powerful teaching tool, but it must be used within a framework of respect and informed consent.

1
Ask explicitly the first time. "Is it okay if I place my hand on your hip to show you the rotation?" Never assume.
2
Consent may become implied as you develop comfort and trust with regular clients ‚Äî but even then, it still needs to be confirmed.
3
Confirm every time you want to use physical contact as a teaching aid. A simple "Can I just show you?" before touching is always enough.
4
Respect a no immediately and without question. If a client declines, move to vocal or mirroring cues without comment.
1
A Tap Creates Awareness
You don't always need to guide or correct ‚Äî sometimes a simple tap on the muscle group you want activated is enough to bring a client's attention there and unlock the movement. Try tapping the hip, the shoulder, or the foot before asking for the movement.
2
Guide Through the Full Range
For rotation especially ‚Äî where clients genuinely cannot feel how little they're moving ‚Äî gently placing hands on the hips and physically guiding the correct rotation lets them feel the target. This is often more effective than any amount of verbal or visual cueing.
3
Use Alongside Other Cues
Physical feedback works best in combination with vocal and visual cues ‚Äî not as a replacement for them. Touch alongside a clear verbal cue creates a multi-sensory input that's far more powerful than any single channel alone.
4
Build Trust First
Physical feedback is most effective once a client trusts you. Invest in the relationship ‚Äî learn their name, remember their challenges, celebrate their progress. When trust is there, a light touch from you carries enormous weight.
