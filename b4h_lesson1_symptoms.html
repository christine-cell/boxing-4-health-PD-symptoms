Stooped Posture
Forward-leaning position causes the centre of gravity to shift forward, resulting in a greater risk of falling. Back pain and overall body stiffness are common companions.
ü•ä Instructor Note: Boxing naturally fights stooped posture ‚Äî guard position requires upright spine and raised arms. Use this as a teaching moment: "This is your boxing posture ‚Äî this is also your best posture."

Shuffling Gait & Freezing
Small, hesitant steps can lead to shuffling gait. Clients tend to walk with their feet very close together, severely compromising balance and increasing fall risk. Freezing episodes (sudden inability to move) can occur especially at doorways or when changing direction.
ü•ä Instructor Note: Marching in place, high knees, and exaggerated stepping drills directly target this. For freezing, teach clients the trick of stepping over an imaginary line or a visual target on the floor.

ü•ä Instructor Note: This is literally what boxing addresses head-on. Cross-body punching movements directly train reciprocal arm swing. Point out improvements to clients ‚Äî "Look how much more your arms are swinging today!"

03 ‚Äî Non-Motor Symptoms

These symptoms are often invisible but can significantly impact a client's ability to participate. Tap any card to reveal instructor tips.

üó£Ô∏è
Quiet Voice & Speech Difficulty
Vocal cord muscle thinning causes a soft, hoarse voice. A gap forms between cords, reducing volume and clarity as the disease progresses.
ü•ä Tip: Always face the client when giving instructions. Never talk over music when coaching someone with voice difficulties. Encourage them to speak loudly during class ‚Äî it's great vocal exercise!
üò£
Painful Muscle Cramps
Often in the feet but can present in different areas of the body. These cramps (dystonias) can be sudden and quite severe.
ü•ä Tip: If a client stops suddenly or appears uncomfortable, ask gently if they're having a cramp. Know your modification for that station. Never push through visible pain.
üò¥
Poor Sleep & Vivid Dreams
Disrupted sleep worsens all other symptoms. Excessive daytime sleepiness, hallucinations, and vivid dreams can occur as the disease progresses.
ü•ä Tip: If a client seems unusually fatigued, reduce intensity without making it obvious. Regular exercise actually improves sleep quality in PD ‚Äî another benefit to share with clients!
üò∞
Depression & Anxiety
Social isolation, increased anxiety, fear, and feeling overwhelmed are very common. Anxiety can directly exacerbate motor symptoms.
ü•ä Tip: Community is medicine. The social environment of your class may be as therapeutic as the exercise itself. Notice if a regular client stops coming ‚Äî reach out. Consistency in attendance is a key health indicator.
üß†
Cognitive Issues & Dementia
Slowness in memory and thinking affects many with PD. Clients may take longer processing directions or not clearly understand an exercise cue.
ü•ä Tip: Break down exercises into simple, one-step cues. Demonstrate before explaining. Be patient ‚Äî repeat instructions calmly and without frustration. Dual-tasking (moving + counting + thinking) is actually excellent cognitive exercise!
ü©∫
Low Blood Pressure
Very common in PD ‚Äî either from the disease itself or medication. Getting up from the floor too quickly can cause dangerous dizziness or fainting.
ü•ä Tip: Always place a chair at every station that involves going to the ground. Teach clients to get up slowly ‚Äî pause seated, then stand. Know your ground-to-stand modifications for every exercise.
üß¥
Skin Problems
Oily or scaly skin can become dry and sensitive. Some clients may not be able to go barefoot in the gym.
ü•ä Tip: Recommend non-slip socks or indoor shoes if needed. Never require bare feet for balance exercises ‚Äî safety first, always.
üåø
Constipation
Very common and often an early non-motor sign of PD. Digestive health is now understood to be a meaningful part of the PD picture.
ü•ä Tip: Exercise promotes gut motility ‚Äî another benefit of your program! Encourage clients to work with their healthcare team on digestive health. You can mention it as something worth discussing with their doctor.

QUESTION 01 / 04A client arrives to class appearing stiff and moving very slowly. It's a morning session. What is the most likely explanation?
AThey didn't sleep well and are tired
BRigidity and bradykinesia are often worst in the morning before medication takes effect
CThey should probably go home and rest
DThey are having a dyskinesia episode
‚úì Correct! Morning stiffness and slowness are classic ‚Äî this is exactly why a thorough warm-up is essential. Give them time, don't rush the first circuits, and watch them loosen up as the session progresses.
The key insight is that rigidity and bradykinesia are typically worst in the morning before medication has had time to work. A proper warm-up is the most important thing you can do for them.
QUESTION 02 / 04A client with PD has a flat, expressionless face throughout class. What should you do?
AAsk if something is wrong ‚Äî they seem unhappy
BGive them easier exercises since they seem disengaged
CUnderstand it's hypomimia (masked expression) ‚Äî check in verbally and treat them normally
DAssume they are in pain and stop the session
‚úì Exactly right. Hypomimia is a symptom of facial muscle stiffness ‚Äî not a reflection of their mood or engagement. Many clients are having the best time even when their face doesn't show it. A simple "How are you feeling?" goes a long way.
A flat expression in PD is called hypomimia ‚Äî it's caused by facial muscle stiffness, not emotions. Never assume a blank face means disengagement or unhappiness. Check in with a simple verbal question.
QUESTION 03 / 04Which of the following is the BEST reason to always have a chair at every station involving floor work?
ASo clients can rest between sets
BIt's required by gym insurance
CLow blood pressure is common in PD ‚Äî getting up too quickly can cause dizziness or fainting
DClients prefer to sit rather than stand
‚úì Perfect. Orthostatic hypotension (blood pressure drop when standing) is very common in PD, caused by both the disease and many medications. A chair at every ground station allows clients to pause and stabilize before standing fully.
The answer is low blood pressure (orthostatic hypotension). When clients go from floor to standing too quickly, blood pressure can drop suddenly causing dizziness or falls. Always have a chair nearby and teach slow, staged getting-up.
QUESTION 04 / 04Why is emphasizing BIG movements throughout every class so important for clients with bradykinesia?
ABig movements burn more calories and improve fitness faster
BIt makes the class look more impressive to observers
CIt trains the brain and body to continue using full, intentional movements outside of class ‚Äî fighting the natural tendency to move smaller
DBig movements are safer for people with balance issues
‚úì Excellent. This is the neurological basis of your program ‚Äî amplitude training. By practicing BIG movements in class, clients are literally retraining their nervous system to counteract bradykinesia. What they practice with you, they carry into daily life.
The key is neurological carryover. Bradykinesia causes movement to get progressively smaller. By training BIG amplitudes in class, you're retraining the brain's movement commands so clients continue to move larger outside of class too.
