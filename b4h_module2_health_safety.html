Incident Reports

‚ö†Ô∏è If Anyone Gets Hurt ‚Äî Here's What to Do

An incident report must be completed as soon as possible after class whenever anyone is injured, has a medical event, or anything unexpected happens. Management must be informed immediately ‚Äî not at your convenience, but ASAP.

1
Attend to the client first. Ensure they are safe and stable. If it is a medical emergency, call 911 immediately before doing anything else.
2
If you are CPR certified and it is needed ‚Äî provide CPR. Use the defibrillator if available and the situation calls for it. You are trained for this.
3
Contact management immediately ‚Äî Head Instructor and/or Gym Manager. Do this as soon as the situation is under control, not the next day.
4
Fill out the incident report as soon as class is over. While details are fresh. Be thorough: time, what happened, who was present, what action was taken, the client's condition when you left.
5
Leave the form in the designated location: folder on the office door at HQ Ottawa, or the communication book at all other locations.
üìã
What counts as an incident? Any fall ‚Äî even if the client says they're fine. Any complaint of chest pain, dizziness, or shortness of breath. Any injury to a joint, muscle, or head. Any medical event of any kind. When in doubt, fill out the report. Over-reporting is never a problem. Under-reporting is.

Hand Wrapping ‚Äî High-Functioning Classes

Proper hand wrapping protects wrist joints and knuckles during bag work. For newer students in high-functioning classes, getting everyone wrapped before class is your responsibility.

1
Arrive early. Wrapping takes time with newer clients ‚Äî don't start class late because you ran out of time to wrap hands. Budget 10‚Äì15 minutes before class begins.
2
Use volunteers. If experienced volunteers or regular clients know how to wrap, delegate to them. It gets done faster and builds community at the same time.
3
Check every pair of hands before class starts. A wrap that's too loose won't protect. Too tight can restrict circulation. Give each wrap a quick check ‚Äî tight enough to support, loose enough to flex.
4
Champion classes: Many Champion clients will not use hand wraps or gloves depending on ability. Assess each client individually ‚Äî focus on safety and comfort over convention.
üí°
Teaching hand wrapping is a great class skill to develop. Over time, most regular clients will learn to wrap their own hands. Teaching them how is a fine motor skill exercise ‚Äî and one more thing they can do independently.

PD-Specific Safety Considerations

Working with Parkinson's clients means being aware of risks that don't apply in general fitness settings. These are the ones to watch for every single class.

ü´∏
Falls ‚Äî The #1 Risk
PD clients have significantly elevated fall risk due to postural instability and freezing of gait. Always ensure clear paths between stations. Never rush transitions. Have chairs available. Assign a spotter for high-balance challenges. Any fall must be documented ‚Äî even if the client says they're fine.
üíä
Medication & OFF Periods
Clients may arrive in an OFF period ‚Äî slower, stiffer, more fatigued than usual. Never push a client through an OFF period. Reduce intensity, offer seated modifications, and monitor closely. If a client's state concerns you, it's always okay to dial back the session.
üòµ
Orthostatic Hypotension
Many PD clients experience a drop in blood pressure when standing up quickly ‚Äî causing dizziness or fainting. Never allow clients to stand up rapidly from seated positions. Always cue slow transitions and watch for signs of dizziness during any sit-to-stand movement.
ü•µ
Overheating & Dehydration
PD affects the body's ability to regulate temperature. Clients may not sweat normally and may not feel thirsty. Proactively prompt water breaks ‚Äî don't wait for clients to ask. Keep the gym well ventilated. Watch for signs of overheating: flushed face, confusion, fatigue beyond what's expected.
ü´Ä
Cardiac Considerations
Some PD clients are on cardiac medications that affect heart rate response. The standard THR calculation may not apply to everyone. Know which clients have cardiac conditions. Use perceived exertion (the talk test) alongside heart rate for these clients. If in doubt, consult with their physician.
üß†
Cognitive Changes
PD can affect processing speed, attention, and spatial awareness. A client who seems confused or disoriented may not be "off" ‚Äî they may be experiencing cognitive symptoms. Slow down, simplify, and check in. If you notice sudden, significant cognitive change, take it seriously and contact management.
