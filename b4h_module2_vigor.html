Voice & Variety

Use it or lose it ‚Äî for real
With Parkinson's, a student's voice can be affected ‚Äî but not everyone experiences this. The voice can become quiet, slow, and hard to understand. The more you use it, the less you lose it. Voice work isn't an add-on ‚Äî it's a therapeutic necessity woven into every class.
It's essential to add vocal exercises during warm-ups and circuits. Create dialogue with your students throughout the session ‚Äî word games, call-and-response, group exercises, name games. Make it fun, make it social, make it loud.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Do NOT start with yelling! The voicebox is a muscle too ‚Äî it needs to be warmed up gradually just like the rest of the body. Start gentle and build intensity through the class.

Voice Exercise Examples
1
Group Hum: Have the class hum a short song together during warm-up ‚Äî Happy Birthday is perfect. See who can hold their hum the longest. This warms up the vocal cords gently and creates instant group energy.
2
Word Games: During warm-up or circuits, play a naming game around the room. Example: "Name car brands using the alphabet ‚Äî A is for Audi, B is for BMW, C is for Chevrolet..." Go around the room, one student per letter. You can also do vegetables, fruits, cities ‚Äî anything that gets the brain and voice working together.
3
Vowel Sounds: Practice A, E, I, O, U ‚Äî making sure the whole face is involved and holding each sound as long as possible. Always demo it yourself first so students feel comfortable making silly sounds and noises. Your willingness to look ridiculous gives them permission to do the same.

ü•ä Variety Matters Too

Variety keeps the brain engaged. Neuroplasticity thrives on novelty ‚Äî doing new things in new ways forces the brain to create new connections. Rotate your voice exercises, word games, and vocal cues so clients are always being pleasantly challenged, never bored.

High intensity isn't just about aerobic fitness ‚Äî it's about the amount of power and output put into every movement. Think about getting off a couch: most of us use adaptations ‚Äî pushing with our hands, using the armrest, or having someone help. What we're actually lacking is the power and intensity to build momentum. That's exactly what we're training.
Intense exercise ‚Äî specifically non-contact boxing ‚Äî has been proven to reverse, reduce, and even delay the symptoms of Parkinson's. We aim to get every client working at 60‚Äì80% of their target heart rate during the high-intensity portion of class. Everyone at every level can benefit. Intensity is not negotiable.

Grand Movements

Go Big or Go Home
Large amplitude, whole-body movements. Everything in class should be done BIG. Big punches, big steps, big arm swings, big facial expressions, big voice. When we use grand gestures and really emphasize size in every movement, we help the brain recalibrate what "normal" feels like.
People with Parkinson's tend to move smaller than they think they are ‚Äî their internal sense of movement size is off. When a PD client tries to move "big" and reaches what feels like 100% effort to them, they might only be at 60% of normal amplitude. Emphasizing grand movements consistently over time helps recalibrate this internal reference point toward normal.

Punches

Full extension on every punch. Arm fully extended, shoulder rotating, weight transferring. No small, tight punches.

üö∂
Steps

Big, deliberate strides. Heel-toe. High knee lifts when appropriate. Exaggerate the stride ‚Äî that shuffle becomes a march.

üôå
Arm Swings

Counter-rotating arm swing with walking and footwork. Reduced arm swing is a hallmark PD symptom ‚Äî train it deliberately.

üòÑ
Facial Expressions

PD causes facial masking ‚Äî reduced expression. Exaggerate vowel sounds, smiles, and surprised faces. The whole face is part of grand movements.

ü•ä Your Job as Instructor

Always demo big. If you do small movements, they do small movements. Your energy and amplitude set the standard for the whole room. When you go big, they go big. Lead with your whole body, every single rep.

O

Overall Rotation

Cervical & Thoracic ‚Äî The Cascade Effect
Rigidity is one of the most universal symptoms of Parkinson's ‚Äî and it's extremely common in seniors too. Clients can experience whole-body stiffness, but the most prominent area is the trunk region. This area has the greatest impact on their ability to rotate ‚Äî and rotation affects everything.

When Trunk Rotation Is Lost...

The shoulders, hips, knees, and back are all forced to overcompensate for what the trunk can't do. This compensation cascade dramatically increases injury risk throughout the entire body ‚Äî not just the back.

What You Must Include

At least one exercise targeting rotation in every functional exercise circuit. And during boxing stations, always cue hip rotation ‚Äî the power of every punch starts in the hips, not the arms.

Most students lack self-awareness about how little they're actually rotating. When you show them the correct range, they think they're already doing it correctly. They genuinely cannot feel the difference.
ü§≤

Hands-on correction is often the only thing that works. Ask the student if you can place your hands on their hips and gently move them through the correct range of motion. Let them feel the difference between what they're doing and what rotation actually is. This often needs to happen multiple times across multiple sessions before it starts to stick ‚Äî and that's completely normal.

Myofascial Release ‚Äî Cool Down with Purpose
Releasing tight fascia improves movement, relieves tightness, and reduces overall tension throughout the body. The cool-down is not just a way to end class ‚Äî it's a targeted therapeutic intervention in its own right.
What Is Fascia?

Fascia is the bands of connective tissue that stabilize, enclose, and separate muscles and organs throughout the entire body. When fascia becomes tight or restricted, it reduces mobility, contributes to pain, and limits the range of motion ‚Äî compounding the rigidity that PD and aging already create.

We use our cool-downs to pick a specific part of the body and work on it with self-massage balls ‚Äî loosening areas that most affect alignment and posture. Rotate through these target areas across different sessions:
